Scaling a Critical Process 680% with Zero Additional Headcount

Redesigning Amazon’s DSP ownership transfer process from ad-hoc firefighting to a structured, data-driven program that projected $25M in annual savings.

Process Design SQL / Redshift Root Cause Analysis Change Management

The Problem

Amazon’s Delivery Service Partner (DSP) ownership transfer process was broken. When I inherited it, there was a 15-case backlog, no documentation, no data infrastructure, and every case was handled ad-hoc. The process took four months per transfer, cost the company through extended exit payments, and had everyone firefighting individual cases.

Previous Annual Volume 5 cases
New Annual Volume 63+ cases

The volume was about to explode: from 5 cases annually to 63+ — a 680% increase — with no additional staff allocated. Without fundamental redesign, the backlog would become unmanageable and the program would collapse.

The Approach

I approached this as a systems problem, not a workload problem. The goal wasn’t to work faster on a broken process — it was to redesign the process so it could scale without breaking.

Three-Phase Strategy

  • Standardize the workflow: Convert ad-hoc case handling into a structured 14-week, 21-milestone framework with clear handoffs and decision points.
  • Build the data infrastructure: Create SQL-based analytics on Amazon Redshift tracking 689 data attributes across 209 candidates — replacing scattered spreadsheets with a single source of truth.
  • Institutionalize the knowledge: Author the CT Process Playbook, create 19 standardized communication templates, and build a defect tracking system with 5-Why root cause analysis.

Key Decisions

Milestone-Based Workflow

The original process had no structure — cases moved forward when someone remembered to push them. I designed a 14-week timeline with 21 specific milestones, each with defined owners, deliverables, and escalation triggers. This turned an amorphous “transfer” into a sequence of discrete, trackable steps.

Weeks 1-2
Initiation & Assessment
Exit notification, candidate identification, initial qualification
Weeks 3-5
Candidate Screening
Background checks, financial verification, interviews
Weeks 6-8
Legal & Contract
Agreement drafting, legal review, stakeholder sign-off
Weeks 9-12
Transition Execution
Knowledge transfer, system access, operational handoff
Weeks 13-14
Close & Monitor
Quality verification, defect tracking, case closure

SQL Analytics on Redshift

The team had been tracking cases in spreadsheets that kept getting corrupted or lost. I built a proper analytics layer on Amazon Redshift, tracking 689 data attributes per candidate across 209 total candidates. This enabled real-time visibility into pipeline health, bottleneck identification, and quality metrics.

Defect Tracking with 5-Why Analysis

When transfers failed post-completion, the team would fix the immediate issue and move on. I implemented systematic 5-Why root cause analysis for every defect, categorizing failures by source and feeding insights back into process improvements. This shifted the team from reactive firefighting to proactive prevention.

Before / After

Before

Ad-hoc case handling. 15-case backlog. No documentation. Scattered spreadsheets. Four-month transfer timeline. Every case a unique firefight. Function viewed as “customer service.”

After

Structured 14-week, 21-milestone framework. Zero backlog. Complete playbook. SQL analytics on 689 attributes. Defect prevention via root cause analysis. Function reframed as “network protection.”

Results

680% Volume Scaling 5 → 63+ cases/year
$25M Projected Savings Annual by 2026
80–100% Post-Transfer Quality
0 Additional Headcount

The program scaled 680% with a single process owner — me — while maintaining 80–100% post-transfer quality. The projected $25M in annual savings came from exit cost optimization: faster, cleaner transfers meant less time paying exiting partners.

Perhaps more importantly, I reframed how leadership viewed the function. It wasn’t “customer service” handling partner requests — it was “network protection” ensuring the delivery network maintained quality during transitions. This reframing changed how the program was valued and resourced.

Lessons Learned

  • Process problems masquerading as workload problems are the most dangerous — throwing more people at a broken process just creates more chaos.
  • The act of creating structure creates capacity. A 21-milestone framework doesn’t just track work; it reveals where work is actually getting stuck.
  • Reframing how leadership sees a function (“customer service” → “network protection”) can matter more than the metrics themselves.
