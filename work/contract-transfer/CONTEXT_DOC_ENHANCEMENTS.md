### What I Inherited

When I took over Contract Transfer operations in Q3 2022, the process was a one-person operation with no infrastructure. Cases lived in email threads and a shared spreadsheet with no standardized stages, no SLAs, and no defined criteria for what "approved" or "rejected" meant. There were 15 active cases sitting in various states of limbo — some had been waiting weeks for a decision, some had incomplete documentation, and the DSP owners on the other end had no visibility into where they stood or what was expected of them.

The previous process had been designed in 2019 for third-party sales — arm's-length transactions where an outside buyer purchases a DSP business. It was a compliance exercise: check the boxes, make a decision. But by 2022, the volume had shifted. More requests were coming from family members, employees, and business partners — people already embedded in the operation who wanted to take ownership. The existing process didn't distinguish between a stranger buying a business and a son taking over his father's.

There was no playbook. No SOP. No communication templates — each rejection, each status update, each approval was written from scratch every time. There was no data infrastructure beyond the spreadsheet, which meant there was no way to identify patterns: why were cases taking 17 weeks? Where were the bottlenecks? Nobody knew, because nobody had built the system to find out.

The 15-case backlog was the symptom. The absence of infrastructure was the problem.

## Enhancement 2: Elevate the Mission Reframe to a Headline Section

**Replaces:** The single bullet in "What I Learned" about mission reframing. Add this as a new section between "My Actions" and "Results."

### The Strategic Reframe: Customer Service to Network Protection

The most important thing I did wasn't a process change or a dashboard — it was reframing what Contract Transfer was for.

When I inherited the program, CT was treated as a customer service function. A DSP owner wanted to sell their business, we processed the request, and success was measured by how fast we closed it and whether the owner was satisfied. The implicit question was: _did we help the DSP owner?_

That framing was wrong. It optimized for the wrong customer.

A DSP contract transfer isn't a favor to the owner — it's a network protection decision. When a DSP exits, it costs Amazon $482K–$504K and degrades Capacity Reliability by 7.7% at that station. Only 39.6% of routes recover. The rest get rerouted at higher cost or diverted to 3P carriers at $7.10 CPP versus AMZL's $5.13. A successful transfer prevents all of that. A bad transfer — approving an unqualified buyer who degrades performance — creates all of that damage plus the cost of an involuntary exit later.

The right question wasn't _"did we help the DSP owner?"_ It was _"did we protect the network?"_

I reframed CT around that question. It changed everything downstream:

| Before (Customer Service Frame)                  | After (Network Protection Frame)                                 |
| ------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Success = fast closure, owner satisfaction       | Success = post-transfer quality maintenance, exit cost avoidance |
| High rejection rate = process failure            | High rejection rate = effective gatekeeping                      |
| 89% rejection rate was a problem to fix          | 89% rejection rate was evidence the bar was calibrated correctly |
| Stakeholders asked "why is this taking so long?" | Stakeholders asked "will the network be protected?"              |
| Volume growth = more work                        | Volume growth = more exits prevented                             |

This reframe is what secured leadership endorsement for the Legacy pathway proposal. When CT was a customer service function, the $25M projection was an interesting number. When CT was a network protection function, it was a risk mitigation imperative.

## Enhancement 3: Add a Specific Case — HHSL

**Add as a new subsection under "Results" or "Key Contributions":**

### Case Example: HHSL — Why the Quality Framework Matters

In March 2023, we approved and launched a contract transfer for HHSL. On paper, it was a success — the new owner cleared vetting, passed the interview loop, and launched on schedule.

Three months later, the quality tracking framework I'd built flagged a deviation. HHSL was missing on one of the seven performance categories: Breach of Contract incidents (BOCs). They were 2 BOCs below the threshold for November — not a catastrophic failure, but a leading indicator. Of the five CT launches in 2023 (HHSL, LGDP, SEDX, UONI, CPAC), HHSL was the only one not meeting the quality standard.

Without the tracking framework, this would have gone unnoticed until the deviation compounded into a real quality problem. Instead, the early warning system caught it, enabling a targeted intervention — operational coaching and closer monitoring — before the deviation became a pattern.

This is exactly what the 21-milestone framework was built for. The easy transfers don't need it. The ones that look easy at launch and develop problems at month three — those are the ones that justify the infrastructure. HHSL validated the quality tracking investment: the framework didn't just measure success, it caught the early signs of failure in time to act.

Four of the five 2023 CT launches maintained quality standards. The one that deviated was caught by the system and corrected. That's an 80% quality rate, +1,930 basis points above goal — and the 20% that missed wasn't missed quietly.

## Enhancement 4: Add the Human Dimension

**Add as a new subsection under "The Situation", after Stakes:**

### What Was at Stake for the DSP Owners

The financial and operational metrics tell one story. The human story is different.

DSP owners are independent business operators. They've built teams — hiring drivers, managing fleets, running daily logistics at Amazon delivery stations. Some have been doing this for years. When a DSP owner reaches the point of wanting to exit, the question isn't just "what happens to the contract?" It's "what happens to the business I built, the team I hired, and the culture I created?"

Forty percent of DSP owners interviewed for the Legacy proposal said they wanted to sustain the business — not just sell it, but pass it to someone who would keep it going. As one CAG member put it: _"I have seen family members that can take over today with no issues, but usually that is not the case and they require more prep."_

The existing process didn't serve that need. A family member or long-time employee who knew the operation inside and out still had to go through the same arm's-length vetting process designed for strangers buying a business. If they didn't pass — and 64% didn't — the owner had two choices: sell to an outsider or exit entirely. Either way, the team, the culture, and the continuity were gone.

The Legacy pathway was designed to close that gap. Not by lowering the bar — the vetting standard stays identical across all acquisition sources — but by giving internal candidates the preparation tools (Road to Ownership content, BC coaching, extended timelines) to meet the bar. The goal was to turn "they could take over if they were ready" into "they are ready."

Meanwhile, the 5% of transfers classified as Survivorship — triggered by a DSP owner's death or incapacitation — added a dimension no process document fully captures. These were families dealing with loss while simultaneously trying to prevent a business from collapsing. The Survivorship SOP I built had to balance urgency (the business needs an owner now) with sensitivity (the family needs time). The 88% owner buy-in rate for Survivorship transfers reflects both: the process worked operationally, and it worked for people.

## Enhancement 6 (Bonus): The Financial Transaction Debate

**Expands:** The single bullet in "What Was Hard" about the "hotly debated" policy question. Add as a subsection in a new "Key Decisions" section.

### The Financial Transaction Question

One of the most contentious policy decisions in the Legacy pathway was whether succession transfers should allow financial transactions between the departing owner and the incoming candidate.

On one side: if a DSP owner built a business worth $200K+, preventing them from selling it to their successor strips the equity they've built. That undermines the entire value proposition of the DSP program — "build a business, build equity." CAG members felt strongly about this. It was a trust issue.

On the other side: if we allow financial transactions in succession transfers, we create a perverse incentive. An owner could inflate the sale price, saddling the new operator with debt that jeopardizes the business's financial sustainability — and by extension, the network's operational reliability. The average 3P transaction was $236K. At that level, debt load becomes a real risk to DSP performance.

My recommendation was to allow transactions but with guardrails: a $30K maximum for Legacy transfers, preserving the "startup ethos" (Amazon's website claims ~$10K startup costs for new DSPs) while acknowledging that some equity transfer is reasonable. Above $30K, the candidate would be flagged for additional financial sustainability review. And we would highlight to all approved candidates that they had the option to launch a net-new DSP at ~$10K as a mechanism to keep transaction values in check — candidates would self-select if the ROI was justified.

This was a compromise. The CAG wanted no cap. Legal wanted no transactions. The $30K threshold with self-selection economics was the middle ground that both sides could commit to. It protected network sustainability while respecting partner equity — and it used market incentives rather than hard caps to regulate behavior.

## Enhancement 7: The Decision SLA — Where 17 Weeks Actually Went

**Add as a new subsection under "My Actions", or as a standalone section between "What I Inherited" and "Key Contributions".**

### 16.98 Weeks to 2.28 Weeks: Anatomy of the SLA Transformation

The headline number — 86.6% reduction in decision time — sounds like a speed optimization. It wasn't. Speed was the outcome. The actual work was making the process legible enough to diagnose, then restructuring it so the diagnosis could become permanent.

#### Where 17 Weeks Went

When I inherited the process, decisions averaged 16.98 weeks. But nobody knew why, because nobody had instrumented the process to find out. The time wasn't spent in one place — it was distributed across dead zones:

- **Undefined handoffs between eight teams.** The process touched BCs, BDMs, Acquisitions, Legal, Network Health, DRR, DSS, and Marketing Technology — but role boundaries were undefined and decision authority was unclear. A case could sit for weeks in a gap between two teams, each assuming the other was acting on it.
- **Expensive vetting stages run on unqualified candidates.** Without front-loaded disqualification criteria, candidates who would ultimately fail were advancing deep into the pipeline — consuming station visits, interview loops, and enhanced vetting hours — before getting rejected at month three for something that could have been caught at week one.
- **No standardized communications.** DSP owners and candidates went weeks without contact. Every status update, every rejection letter, every approval notification was written from scratch. That created delay (someone had to draft it) and inconsistency (every message read differently).
- **No SLAs, no tracking, no accountability.** Without defined stage durations, there was no way to know a case was falling behind until someone complained. And without data infrastructure, there was no way to identify which stage was the bottleneck — the answer was always "it's just a slow process."

The 16.98-week average wasn't a speed problem. It was a visibility problem. You can't fix what you can't see.

#### What I Built: The 14-Week, 21-Milestone Framework

I redesigned the entire workflow into five sequential phases with defined SLAs, explicit ownership, and documented handoff criteria:

| Phase               | What Happens                                                                         | SLA       |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------- |
| **1. Notification** | BC notifies CT team of owner intent; initial eligibility screen                      | 48 hours  |
| **2. Intake**       | Application submitted; 6-dimension rubric review; automatic disqualifiers applied    | 1 week    |
| **3. Vetting**      | Background/credit check → HireVue → Enhanced Vetting (DRR) → Station visit           | 4-6 weeks |
| **4. Approvals**    | 5-stakeholder consensus model (BC Mgr, Acquisitions Mgr, NH Mgr, Legal, NH Director) | 2 weeks   |
| **5. Onboarding**   | Launch coordination, operational readiness, first 90-day quality monitoring          | 4 weeks   |

The critical design decision was **front-loading the cheapest gates first**. The vetting sequence was ordered by cost:

1. Application review against 6-dimension rubric (minutes, automatic disqualifiers defined)
2. Background and credit check (days, low cost)
3. HireVue video assessment (days, moderate cost)
4. Enhanced vetting via DRR (1-2 weeks, higher cost)
5. Station visit (travel, coordination)
6. Interview loop (most expensive — multiple senior stakeholders' time)

This sequencing meant the 89% of candidates who didn't qualify were identified in days, not months — before the expensive stages. A candidate who would fail on a credit check was no longer consuming a station visit and an interview loop three months later.

#### The Defect Tracking System

Once the framework was live, I built the analytics to sustain it. The SQL infrastructure tracking 689 data attributes across 209 candidate records enabled the first root cause analysis of where the process actually failed. Six defect categories emerged:

| Defect Category          | % of Total Defects | What It Means                                                                                     |
| ------------------------ | ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Credit Check Delay       | 56%                | Credit checks not completing in time, stalling the application review that required them as input |
| Phone Scheduling Backlog | 20%                | Candidates waiting for phone screens; scheduling bottleneck                                       |
| EV Sequence Errors       | 12%                | Enhanced Vetting steps executed out of order                                                      |
| Decision SLA Breach      | 12%                | A stage exceeded its defined time window                                                          |

These weren't intuitive problems. You couldn't have guessed that 56% of defects were a single upstream dependency — credit checks not completing in time, stalling the application review that couldn't proceed without them. That finding only existed because the data infrastructure existed. And once it was visible, it was fixable.

#### The Results

| Metric                | 2022 (Inherited) | 2023 (After Framework)   | Change                                |
| --------------------- | ---------------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------------- |
| Average decision time | 16.98 weeks      | 2.28 weeks               | -86.6%                                |
| Median decision time  | 9.6 weeks        | 2.9 weeks                | -70%                                  |
| Goal SLA              | 8 weeks          | 8 weeks                  | —                                     |
| Performance vs. goal  | 8.98 weeks over  | 5.72 weeks under         | —                                     |
| Longest case          | Unknown          | CATY at DFL7: 8.71 weeks | Even the outlier beat the old average |
| December requests     | —                | 0.85 weeks               | Sub-1-week processing achieved        |
| Cases processed       | ~5/year          | 28 (2023 YTD)            | While improving speed                 |
| MoM improvement       | —                | +0.08 weeks/month        | Continuous optimization               |

The longest case in 2023 — CATY at DFL7, which took 8.71 weeks and involved a prospective buyer who passed at the final approval stage — was still faster than the _average_ decision time before the framework existed. December requests were closing in under a week.

Decision time dropped 86.6% not because the bar was lowered, but because stage gates finally caught unqualified candidates in days instead of months — and because the process was visible enough to diagnose and fix the bottlenecks that had always been there.

#### Why This Matters for the Legacy Pathway

The SLA transformation wasn't just an operational win — it was a prerequisite for the Legacy strategy. You can't credibly propose a $25M succession initiative built on top of a process that takes 17 weeks to make a decision. The 2.28-week average proved the infrastructure could handle scale. It proved that quality and speed weren't in tension — the same front-loading that cut time also improved quality, because bad candidates were filtered earlier instead of consuming resources and creating false hope for months.

The SLA data also became the evidence base for the Legacy proposal itself. When I presented to leadership, I could show: the process works at speed, it works at quality, and it works at scale. The question wasn't "can we do this?" — it was "how much value are we leaving on the table by not doing it?" The answer was $25M.
