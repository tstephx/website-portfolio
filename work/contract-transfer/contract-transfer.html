From Zero Documentation to 87 Cases a Year — Solo

I inherited Amazon’s DSP ownership transfer process with no SOP, no communication framework, and no data infrastructure — then designed the complete operational system that’s prevented $13M in exit costs.

Every Failed Transfer Cost Half a Million Dollars

Amazon delivers billions of packages through a network of independent Delivery Service Partners (DSPs) — small business owners who operate fleets of vans and drivers under the Amazon brand. When a DSP owner needs to exit, their business must be transferred to a new owner without disrupting deliveries. The stakes cut both ways: a failed exit costs Amazon $482K–$504K in backfill, onboarding, and ramp costs, while dropping network capacity by 7.7% with only 39.6% of routes recovered. But transferring to the wrong person is equally dangerous — a new owner who can’t maintain capacity reliability, safety standards, or financial viability ends up exiting involuntarily, triggering the same $482K+ cost all over again.

When I inherited this process, it was broken. There was no SOP, no standardized communications — just 4 emails to candidates and zero to DSP owners across the entire lifecycle — no formalized vetting criteria, and heavy reliance on tribal knowledge across 6 teams. One stakeholder summarized it bluntly: “There’s not much… it’s an area we just need to be more attentive. It’s been disappointing.”

Annual Transfer Requests: 2020 vs. 2024

Volume was exploding: from 5 transfer requests in 2020 to 87 in 2024 — a 17x increase — with no additional staff. Transfers took 4+ months. Without fundamental redesign, the program would collapse under its own weight.

This wasn’t a workload problem — it was a design problem. The process had to solve two things that pull in opposite directions: move fast enough that DSP owners don’t exit before a transfer completes, but vet rigorously enough that the replacement can actually run the business. Speed without quality is worse than no transfer at all.

Designing a System, Not Patching a Process

I approached this as a service design challenge. The goal wasn’t to work faster on a broken process — it was to architect a system that could scale without breaking. I mapped the full lifecycle end-to-end, identified every handoff and decision point, and built infrastructure where none existed.

Three-Phase Strategy

  • Design the process architecture: Codify the ad-hoc workflow into a structured 14-week lifecycle (5 weeks vetting + 2 weeks approvals + 7 weeks onboarding) with defined SLAs, stage gates, and 3 distinct transfer-type paths.
  • Build the data infrastructure: Create SQL-based analytics on Amazon Redshift tracking 689 data attributes across 209 candidates — replacing scattered spreadsheets with a single source of truth.
  • Institutionalize the knowledge: Author a comprehensive SOP with 13 appendices, 19 communication templates, a BC/BDM Playbook, vetting rubrics, and a governance model — every artifact standalone so any team member could execute without tribal knowledge.

Three Transfer Types, One Architecture

Not all transfers carry the same risk. I defined 3 distinct use cases with differentiated vetting paths, approval criteria, and communication flows. The DSP program is highly selective — transfer candidates are held to the same bar as every other network owner. That’s why 3rd-party candidates have a 79% rejection rate: the vetting exists to protect the network, not to process paperwork.

Stakeholder Governance

The transfer process spanned 6 teams — BCs (first contact), BDMs (candidate relationship), NH (supervision), Acquisitions (vetting execution), DRR (enhanced screening), and Legal (agreement review). Each had different requirements and unclear boundaries, causing inconsistent candidate experiences and confusion about decision authority. I defined explicit roles for each team and designed a 5-stakeholder consensus model for approvals — a governance layer unique to CT that doesn’t exist in standard DSP recruiting.

From Tribal Knowledge to 13 Appendices

14-Week Process Architecture

The inherited process had no timeline, no stage gates, and no defined handoffs. I designed a 5-phase lifecycle with SLAs at every stage — the first time the CT process had a documented, repeatable architecture.

Phase 1
Notification & Intake
DSP Owner notifies BC of intent. CT PM validates eligibility (no open BOCs, not at-risk). Candidate completes application + Buyer Supplementary Questionnaire — a new artifact I designed for CT-specific data collection.
Phase 2 — 5 weeks
Vetting (6 Steps)
Application Review (6-dimension rubric: leadership, coaching ability, logistics experience, customer obsession, grit) → Background/Credit Check → HireVue (10 questions) → Enhanced Vetting → Station Visit → Interview Loop (3×45 min, 6 competencies). Front-loaded to catch disqualifiers early — 34% rejected within 2 weeks at the cheapest gates, before Amazon invests in expensive later stages.
Phase 3 — 2 weeks
Approvals (100% Consensus)
5-stakeholder panel: BC Manager, Acquisitions Manager, NH Manager, Legal, NH Director. Interview pass ≠ acceptance — a deliberate design difference from standard DSP recruiting.
Phase 4 — 7 weeks
Onboarding & Transition
Conditional offer via Ownership Transfer Agreement. DSPi & OEW training. 45-day minimum supportive transition period. Fleet transferred as-is.
Continuous
Communications (19 Templates)
No party goes more than 2 weeks without contact. Separate tracks for DSP Owners and Candidates. Legally reviewed language at every touchpoint.

Communication Transformation

Before the redesign: 4 standardized emails to candidates. Zero to DSP owners. Both parties experienced extended silence with no visibility into status, next steps, or expectations. I authored 19 templates covering every lifecycle state — from initial inquiry through conditional approval — with 2-week cadence updates during inactive periods and post-rejection talking points addressing 7 escalation scenarios (appeals, discrimination claims, selling without approval).

Median Decision Time by Year (Weeks)

Median time to decision dropped 70%, from 9.6 weeks in 2021 to 2.9 weeks in 2023, as the SOP standardized each stage. Three processing tiers emerged from the data: Fast Track (34% of cases, decision within 2 weeks at the cheapest gates), Standard (28%, 2–6 weeks through full vetting), and Extended (38%, 6+ weeks for investigation-level complexity).

The 29 completed transfers prevented an estimated $13M in exit-related costs. Each successful transfer eliminates the $482K–$504K cost of a DSP exit and preserves 100% of network capacity — compared to the 7.7% capacity drop and 60.4% permanent route loss that comes with an unmanaged exit. But the real proof that the vetting works: Legacy transferees maintained quality at +1,800 basis points above the network average. The process didn’t just move faster — it selected better.

The strongest validation came in 2025: the Legacy strategy document — a leadership-level initiative to formalize how DSPs transition ownership to internal candidates — explicitly built on this SOP as its foundational infrastructure. I was named owner of 3 of 4 implementation workstreams. The system I designed didn’t just solve the immediate problem — it became the platform for the program’s next phase.

What This Taught Me

  • Process problems masquerading as workload problems are the most dangerous — throwing more people at a broken process just creates more chaos. The answer was infrastructure, not headcount.
  • The act of creating structure creates capacity. A 5-phase lifecycle with stage gates doesn’t just track work — it reveals where work gets stuck and where handoffs fail.
  • Design for the edge cases first. The survivorship framework (4 sub-scenarios for owner death or incapacitation) forced decisions that simplified the standard path.
  • Reframing how leadership sees a function can matter more than the metrics. “Customer service” implies processing requests. “Network protection” implies guarding against both exit losses and bad replacements. That reframe changed how the program was resourced — and led to the Legacy strategy adoption.
