Scaling a Critical Process 680% with Zero Additional Headcount

Redesigning Amazon’s DSP ownership transfer process from ad-hoc firefighting to a structured, data-driven program that projected $25M in annual savings.

Process Design SQL / Redshift Root Cause Analysis Change Management
Timeline 2023–2025
My Role Sole Process Owner
Stakeholders 14+ groups (Legal, Finance, Ops, Field, HR)
Data Scale 689 attributes × 209 candidates

Every Transfer Was a Firefight

Amazon delivers billions of packages through a network of independent Delivery Service Partners (DSPs) — small business owners who operate fleets of vans and drivers under the Amazon brand. When a DSP owner needs to exit, their business must be transferred to a new owner without disrupting deliveries. Think of it like a franchise transfer: legal, financial, and operational handoffs all have to happen in sequence, and a delay in any one area stalls the entire chain.

When I inherited this process, it was broken. There was a 15-case backlog, no documentation, no data infrastructure, and every case was handled ad-hoc. The process took roughly 17 weeks per transfer, cost the company through extended exit payments averaging $236K per partner, and had everyone firefighting individual cases.

Volume Scaling: Before vs. After

The volume was about to explode: from 5 cases annually to 63+ — a 680% increase — with no additional staff allocated. Each delayed transfer cost Amazon an average of $236K in extended exit payments. Without fundamental redesign, the backlog would become unmanageable and the program would collapse under its own weight.

This wasn’t a workload problem. Throwing more people at a broken process just creates more chaos. It was a systems problem — and the system didn’t exist yet.

Building Infrastructure Where None Existed

I approached this as a systems problem, not a staffing problem. The goal wasn’t to work faster on a broken process — it was to design a process that could scale without breaking. I mapped the full lifecycle end-to-end, identified every handoff and decision point, and built infrastructure where none existed.

Three-Phase Strategy

  • Standardize the workflow: Convert ad-hoc case handling into a structured 14-week, 21-milestone framework with clear handoffs, defined owners, and escalation triggers at every decision point.
  • Build the data infrastructure: Create SQL-based analytics on Amazon Redshift tracking 689 data attributes across 209 candidates — replacing scattered spreadsheets with a single source of truth that updated in real time.
  • Institutionalize the knowledge: Author the CT Process Playbook, create 19 standardized communication templates, and build a defect tracking system with 5-Why root cause analysis going 5–8 layers deep on every failure.

Stakeholder Coordination

The transfer process touched 14+ stakeholder groups — Legal, Finance, Field Operations, HR, Partner Management, and more. Each had different requirements and timelines. I designed the milestone framework to synchronize these groups, creating shared visibility into where each case stood and what each team needed to deliver next. Without this alignment, the 680% volume increase would have overwhelmed every downstream team.

From Ad-Hoc to 21 Milestones

Milestone-Based Workflow

The original process had no structure — cases moved forward when someone remembered to push them. I designed a 14-week timeline with 21 specific milestones, each with defined owners, deliverables, and escalation triggers. This turned an amorphous “transfer” into a sequence of discrete, trackable steps.

Weeks 1–2
Initiation & Assessment
Exit notification, candidate identification, initial qualification screening
Weeks 3–5
Candidate Screening
Background checks, financial verification, panel interviews, scoring
Weeks 6–8
Legal & Contract
Agreement drafting, legal review, stakeholder sign-off across 14+ groups
Weeks 9–12
Transition Execution
Knowledge transfer, system access, operational handoff, field coordination
Weeks 13–14
Close & Monitor
Quality verification, defect tracking, 5-Why analysis on any issues, case closure

SQL Analytics on Redshift

The team had been tracking cases in spreadsheets that kept getting corrupted or lost. I built a proper analytics layer on Amazon Redshift, tracking 689 data attributes per candidate across 209 total candidates. This enabled real-time visibility into pipeline health, bottleneck identification, and quality metrics — replacing a system where nobody could answer basic questions like “How many cases are in progress right now?”

Defect Tracking with 5-Why Analysis

When transfers failed post-completion, the team would fix the immediate issue and move on. I implemented systematic 5-Why root cause analysis for every defect, going 5–8 layers deep on each failure. I categorized failures by source and fed insights back into process improvements. This shifted the team from reactive firefighting to proactive prevention — the same defect never happened twice.

Cycle Time: Before vs. After

The program scaled 680% with a single process owner — me — while maintaining 80–100% post-transfer quality. Cycle time dropped 86.6%, from roughly 17 weeks to approximately 2 weeks per transfer. The $13M in exit costs prevented came from faster, cleaner transfers that reduced extended exit payments across 29 completed cases. The broader $25M projection captures the annualized impact of the program operating at scale.

Perhaps more importantly, I reframed how leadership viewed the function. It wasn’t “customer service” handling partner requests — it was “network protection” ensuring the delivery network maintained quality during transitions. This reframing changed how the program was valued, resourced, and prioritized across the organization.

What This Taught Me

  • Process problems masquerading as workload problems are the most dangerous — throwing more people at a broken process just creates more chaos. The answer was infrastructure, not headcount.
  • The act of creating structure creates capacity. A 21-milestone framework doesn’t just track work; it reveals where work is actually getting stuck and where handoffs are failing.
  • Reframing how leadership sees a function (“customer service” → “network protection”) can matter more than the metrics themselves. The reframe changed how the program was resourced and prioritized.
  • Going 5–8 layers deep on root cause analysis is what separates lasting fixes from temporary patches. Surface-level fixes feel productive but leave the underlying system broken.
