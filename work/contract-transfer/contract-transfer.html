A $482K+ Problem No One Had Written Down

The DSP Acquisitions team needed a way to transition business ownership—one that preserved delivery network integrity while providing owners an equity exit path. Every failed transfer became a DSP exit. Every exit reduced network capacity by 7.7%, recovering only 39.6% of routes. The business was absorbing $482K+ per exit that a functioning program could have prevented.

The Decision That Changed the Program

In Q3 2022, I had two ways to frame the program’s purpose.

Option A — Rejected
CT as Customer Service
Success = requests processed, owners satisfied. Under this framing, 89% rejection is damning—the program is failing most of the people it serves.
Option B — Chosen
CT as Network Protection
Success = network stays intact, bad transfers don’t happen. Under this framing, 89% rejection is evidence the program is working—the vetting catches who it should.

I chose Option B—and built the entire operating system around it. Framing CT as network protection meant defending high rejection rates to stakeholders who would have preferred faster approvals. It meant measuring success in exit costs avoided, not cases closed. The program’s value was invisible when it was working correctly—a prevented exit leaves no trace.

But it was the only framing that made the SOP defensible. The only framing that could justify a 100% consensus approval model. And the only framing under which I could argue—successfully, to leadership—that CT deserved to become formal succession infrastructure.

What Changed

70% faster decisions. Here’s how.

Decisions that took 9.6 weeks now take 2.9—a 70% reduction. I designed a 14-week, 21-milestone framework spanning five phases: Notification → Intake → Vetting → Approvals → Onboarding. Each phase had defined SLAs, explicit ownership assignments, and documented handoff criteria.

The vetting sequence was front-loaded by design—cheapest gates first. The 89% who didn’t qualify were identified in days, not months—before the expensive stages. The inherited rejection rate of 89% improved to 79% post-revamp—approval rate nearly doubled from 11% to 21%.

Process attributes tracked. Six defect categories identified.

I built a SQL-based analytics system tracking internal and external user and process attributes across candidate records—enabling the first root-cause analysis of where the process actually failed. Weekly review dashboards gave stakeholders the first real-time view of pipeline health.

Defect Category Breakdown

19 templates. Zero unexplained silences.

Extended silences during business transfers were driving escalations. I authored 19 email templates covering both DSP owners and candidates across the full lifecycle—separate tracks, separate tone, legally reviewed language throughout. Moving from 4 candidate-only emails to full coverage eliminated the silences and protected the company’s position on every rejection.

The Playbook—end-to-end process mapping.

I authored the CT Process Playbook: end-to-end process mapping with rubrics, communications, workflows, and timelines. It included role boundaries and talking points for field teams, a survivorship framework covering 4 sub-scenarios with graduated vetting, and the application review rubric with candidate tiering. The program’s survival no longer depended on any single person.

100% consensus required.

I designed a 5-stakeholder consensus approval model—field coach manager, Acquisitions manager, operations manager, Legal, operations director—requiring unanimous agreement before the team confirmed any transfer. No single point of decision failure.

The SOP became the foundation for the 2025 Legacy Transfer strategy—a leadership-level initiative to formalize internal succession pathways, targeting a 70% approval rate, 72 annual DSP exits reduced to 20, and $25M in avoided costs by 2026. I own three of four implementation workstreams.

Lessons Worth Stealing

  • Question the metric before questioning the program. If 89% rejection looks like failure, ask whether the framing is wrong before assuming the process is.
  • Front-load the cheapest gates. The 89% who don’t qualify should be identified in days, not months—before the expensive stages consume everyone’s time.
  • A process held together by one person’s tribal knowledge is not a process—it’s a liability. If the program dies when the person leaves, you don’t have a program.
  • If you can’t prove the process works with data, you can’t defend it to leadership. The reframe only held because the analytics made it provable.
